Introduction

Types of Ionizing Radiation

Ionizing radiation carries enough energy to remove electrons from atoms, creating ions and breaking molecular bonds — including DNA. This activity covers the four major types.

Alpha Radiation

Alpha (α) Particle

Composed of 2 protons + 2 neutrons — identical to a helium-4 nucleus. Emitted by heavy unstable nuclei such as Uranium-238, Radium-226, and Polonium-210 (Bushberg et al., 2021).

Charge
+2
Ionizing Power
Very High
Penetration
Low
Shield
Paper / Skin
🩺 Health Effects: External alpha exposure poses minimal risk because dead skin cells block the particles. However, if alpha emitters are inhaled or ingested, the particles cause intense local ionization directly on sensitive tissue — dramatically increasing risk of lung cancer (radon) or internal organ damage (Bushberg et al., 2021; U.S. EPA, 2023).
🌍 Real-World Examples: Radon-222 gas seeping into homes (leading cause of lung cancer among non-smokers); Uranium in nuclear fuel; Polonium-210 used in the 2006 poisoning of Alexander Litvinenko (Goldfarb & Litvinenko, 2007; U.S. EPA, 2023).

(Bushberg et al., 2021; U.S. EPA, 2023; Goldfarb & Litvinenko, 2007)

⚡
Beta Radiation

Beta (β) Particle

High-speed electrons (β⁻) or positrons (β⁺) emitted during nuclear decay. Sources include Carbon-14, Strontium-90, and Iodine-131 (Bushberg et al., 2021).

Charge
−1 or +1
Ionizing Power
Moderate
Penetration
Moderate
Shield
Plastic / Aluminum
🩺 Health Effects: Beta particles penetrate outer skin layers and can cause radiation burns, cataracts (prolonged eye exposure), and increased risk of leukemia and solid tumors. Strontium-90 mimics calcium, accumulating in bone and irradiating marrow — elevating risk of bone cancer (UNSCEAR, 2020).
🌍 Real-World Examples: The 1986 Chernobyl disaster released Iodine-131, causing thyroid cancer in children across Ukraine and Belarus. Today, I-131 is used therapeutically to destroy cancerous thyroid tissue — the same property that makes it dangerous also makes it medically useful (NCI, 2022; WHO, 2023).

(Bushberg et al., 2021; UNSCEAR, 2020; NCI, 2022; WHO, 2023)

🌊
Gamma Radiation

Gamma (γ) Ray

High-energy electromagnetic photons emitted from excited nuclei following alpha or beta decay. Sources include Cobalt-60, Cesium-137, and naturally occurring Potassium-40 (Bushberg et al., 2021).

Charge
0 (photon)
Ionizing Power
Low–Moderate
Penetration
Very High
Shield
Lead / Thick Concrete
🩺 Health Effects: Gamma rays pass through the entire body, posing a whole-body exposure hazard. High doses cause acute radiation syndrome (nausea, hair loss, organ damage). Long-term low-dose exposure increases risk of cancer, particularly leukemia. Because they have no mass or charge, dense materials are required to attenuate them (WHO, 2023; UNSCEAR, 2020).
🌍 Real-World Examples: The 1987 Goiânia accident (Brazil) involved a stolen Cesium-137 source, exposing hundreds and killing four people (IAEA, 1988). Cobalt-60 is used in radiation therapy to treat cancer, and in food irradiation. Background gamma radiation from K-40 is present in soil, building materials, and the human body (IAEA, 1988; NCI, 2022).

(Bushberg et al., 2021; IAEA, 1988; NCI, 2022; WHO, 2023; UNSCEAR, 2020)

🔵
Neutron Radiation

Neutron (n)

Electrically neutral particles emitted during nuclear fission. Neutrons can convert stable materials into radioactive ones through neutron activation (IAEA, 2000).

Charge
0 (neutral)
Ionizing Power
High (indirect)
Penetration
Very High
Shield
Water / Paraffin / Polyethylene
🩺 Health Effects: Neutrons penetrate deeply and interact with nuclei in tissue. They can cause acute radiation syndrome at high doses and significantly elevate cancer risk. Neutrons cause indirect ionization by producing secondary charged particles (protons, gamma) inside the body. They can also make implanted metals temporarily radioactive (IAEA, 2000; UNSCEAR, 2020).
🌍 Real-World Examples: The 1999 Tokaimura criticality accident (Japan) released a burst of neutron radiation, fatally exposing two workers to lethal doses (IAEA, 2000). Nuclear reactors produce intense neutron fields; water surrounding fuel rods serves as both moderator and shield. Neutron radiation is also used in neutron radiography for non-destructive testing of materials (IAEA, 2000).

(IAEA, 2000; UNSCEAR, 2020; Bushberg et al., 2021)
