Background

What is Ionizing Radiation?

Ionizing radiation carries enough energy to remove electrons from atoms creating ions. This distinguishes it from non-ionizing radiation like visible light or radio waves.

Minimum Energy
~10 eV to ionize
Key Effect
Breaks molecular bonds
Types
Particle & Electromagnetic
Primary Concern
Biological damage to DNA
⚛️ Two categories: Particulate radiation (alpha, beta, neutrons) consists of physical particles. Electromagnetic radiation (gamma, X-rays) consists of photons traveling at the speed of light. Both can ionize atoms, but they differ greatly in penetrating power and shielding requirements (World Health Organization [WHO], 2023).

(WHO, 2023; National Cancer Institute [NCI], 2022)

Types Overview

The Four Main Types

⚫
Alpha (α)
PARTICLE RADIATION · He4 NUCLEUS
Heaviest, most ionizing. Stopped by a sheet of paper or skin. Dangerous if inhaled or ingested.
⚡
Beta (β)
PARTICLE RADIATION · ELECTRON / POSITRON
Moderate penetration. Stopped by plastic, glass, or thin aluminum. Can cause skin burns.
🌊
Gamma (γ)
ELECTROMAGNETIC · HIGH ENERGY PHOTON
Most penetrating. Requires dense lead or thick concrete to attenuate. Whole-body exposure risk.
🔵
Neutron (n)
PARTICLE RADIATION · UNCHARGED
Highly penetrating, induces radioactivity. Shielded by hydrogen-rich materials (water, polyethylene).
⚫
Alpha Radiation

Alpha (α) Particle

Composed of 2 protons + 2 neutrons identical to a helium-4 nucleus. Emitted by heavy nuclei like Uranium-238, Radium-226, and Polonium-210.

Charge
+2
Mass (amu)
~4
Penetration
Low
Ionizing Power
Very High
Speed
~5% of c
Shield
Paper / Skin
🛡️ Hazard context: Although easily stopped externally, alpha emitters become extremely dangerous if inhaled or ingested direct contact with sensitive tissue causes intense local ionization. Example: Radon gas in homes is a leading cause of lung cancer.
⚡
Beta Radiation

Beta (β) Particle

High-speed electrons (β⁻) or positrons (β⁺) emitted during nuclear decay. Sources include Carbon-14, Strontium-90, and Iodine-131 (Bushberg et al., 2021).

Charge
−1 or +1
Penetration
Moderate (skin and tissue)
🩹 Health Effects: Beta particles can penetrate the outer skin layers and reach living tissue beneath, causing radiation burns, cataracts with prolonged eye exposure, and increased lifetime risk of leukemia and solid cancers with sufficient dose. Strontium-90, a fission product released during nuclear weapons testing and reactor accidents, mimics calcium and accumulates in bone tissue, irradiating bone marrow and elevating risk of bone cancer and leukemia (United Nations Scientific Committee on the Effects of Atomic Radiation [UNSCEAR], 2020).
🏥 Real-World Example and Medical Use: Following the 1986 Chernobyl disaster, widespread environmental contamination with Iodine-131 (a beta emitter) caused a dramatic rise in thyroid cancer among children in Ukraine and Belarus (WHO, 2006). Today, that same property is harnessed therapeutically: Iodine-131 is administered orally to selectively destroy cancerous thyroid tissue in patients with thyroid cancer, illustrating how beta radiation serves both as a hazard and a medical tool (NCI, 2022).

(Bushberg et al., 2021; UNSCEAR, 2020; WHO, 2006; NCI, 2022)

🌊
Gamma Radiation

Gamma (γ) Ray

High-energy electromagnetic photons emitted from excited nuclei after alpha or beta decay. Sources: Cobalt-60, Cesium-137, naturally occurring K-40.

Charge
0 (photon)
Mass
0 (massless)
Penetration
Very High
Ionizing Power
Low to Moderate
Speed
Speed of light
Shield
Lead / Concrete
☢️ Key insight: Gamma rays pass through the human body easily, making external exposure a whole-body hazard. Because they have no charge or mass, they are harder to stop than particles requiring dense materials like lead for effective shielding.
🔵
Neutron Radiation

Neutron (n)

Electrically neutral particles emitted during fission reactions and in nuclear reactors. Neutrons can make stable materials radioactive through neutron activation.

Charge
0 (neutral)
Mass (amu)
~1
Penetration
Very High
Ionizing Power
High (indirect)
Source
Reactors / Fission
Shield
Water / Paraffin
⚡ Unique hazard: Because neutrons are electrically neutral, they pass through matter easily and interact with nuclei directly. This can cause neutron activation converting stable materials into radioactive ones. Hydrogen-rich materials are the best shield because neutrons lose energy through elastic collisions with protons.
